What are Blood Tests?

Blood tests are common medical tests. You may have a blood test as part of a routine physical examination or because you have certain symptoms.

There are many different blood tests. Some tests focus on your blood cells and platelets. Some evaluate substances in your blood such as electrolytes, proteins, and hormones. Others measure certain minerals in your blood.

Regardless of why you’re having a blood test, it’s important to remember that blood tests help healthcare providers diagnose health issues. But blood test results aren’t diagnoses. An abnormal blood test result may not mean you have a serious medical condition.

When would I need to have a blood test?

Your blood plays a big role in your overall health and contains a lot of information about what may be going on in your body. That’s one reason why blood tests are a common medical test. A healthcare provider may do a blood test because:

  • It’s time for your regular physical: During a checkup, your provider may order blood tests to check on your overall health.
  • Your provider recommends screening tests: Screening tests are done before you have any symptoms.
  • You don’t feel well: If you have specific symptoms, your provider may order blood tests to determine what’s causing them.
  • You have a medical condition that happens when certain genes change: Your blood cells and platelets may show information about specific changes.
  • You’re receiving treatment for a medical condition: Regular blood tests can help assess if treatment is working.
  • You may have inherited certain genetic mutations: Blood samples for genetic analysis can indicate risks for specific conditions.

What do blood tests show?

In a broad sense, a blood test shows changes in your body. Blood test results don’t show a complete picture; instead, they’re a kind of snapshot.

  • The tests show if your blood is working as it should, such as detecting anemia through low red blood cell levels.
  • They indicate normal levels of enzymes and electrolytes, which are crucial for various bodily functions.

What are the most common blood tests?

Some common tests include:

  • Complete Blood Count (CBC): Counts and measures red and white blood cells and platelets.
  • Basic Metabolic Panel (BMP): Measures substances like glucose, calcium, and electrolytes.
  • Comprehensive Metabolic Panel (CMP): Provides more extensive information than BMP.
  • Electrolyte Panels: Checks levels of key electrolytes in the body.

What does a Complete Blood Count (CBC) test show?

This is the most common blood test that includes several specialized tests:

  • Count and measure red and white blood cells and platelets.
  • Measure hemoglobin levels.
  • Assess variations in red blood cell size with RDW (Red Cell Distribution Width).
  • Measure average size of red blood cells with MCV (Mean Corpuscular Volume).

What does a Basic Metabolic Panel show?

A BMP measures several substances in your blood to evaluate overall health:

  • Blood glucose test: Screens for diabetes.
  • Calcium test: Checks calcium levels for various functions.
  • BUN test: Measures urea nitrogen levels from kidneys.
  • Creaine kinase (CK) test: Screens for muscle damage indicators.
  • Sodium levels and CO2 measurements to detect carbon dioxide levels.
  • Potasium and chloride tests to assess electrolyte balance.
  • Globulin test: Measures liver-produced protein levels.

Comprehensive Metabolic Panel and Blood Tests

What does a Comprehensive Metabolic Panel show?

Comprehensive metabolic panels (CMP) include all the blood tests done as part of a basic metabolic panel. Additional blood tests include:

  • Albumin blood test: Checks on kidney and liver function.
  • Alanine transaminase (ALT): Assesses liver health.
  • Alkaline phosphatase (ALP): High levels may indicate liver disease or certain bone disorders.
  • Ammonia levels: High ammonia levels may be a sign of liver and kidney damage.
  • Bilirubin blood test: Too much bilirubin may indicate liver issues.
  • Aspartate transferase (AST): Measures enzyme levels to assess liver health.

What does an Electrolyte Panel show?

Electrolytes are minerals in your blood, and an electrolyte panel includes all electrolyte tests in BMPs and CMPs. Additional levels tested include:

  • Magnesium: Supports brain, heart, and muscle function.
  • Anion gap: Checks the acid-base balance in your blood.

Blood Tests for Specific Conditions

While various blood tests provide a lot of information, some are disease-specific to help diagnose conditions:

Allergies

An allergy blood test checks for increased levels of immunoglobulin E (IgE) antibodies to detect allergies to foods, pets, pollen, etc.

Autoimmune Diseases

  • Antinuclear antibody test: Indicates certain autoimmune disorders.
  • CE complement blood test: Monitors autoimmune disorders like rheumatoid arthritis or lupus.
  • C-reactive protein (CRP) test: High levels may indicate inflammatory conditions.
  • Erythrocyte sedimentation rate (ESR): Helps detect inflammation.
  • Peripheral blood smear (PBS): Examines red and white blood cells under a microscope.

Cancer/Noncancerous Blood Disorders

Cancer blood tests fall into four categories: complete blood count, tumor markers, blood protein testing, and circulating tumor tests.

Complete Blood Count (CBC)

A CBC measures red and white blood cell and platelet levels. Abnormal levels may indicate cancer.

Tumor Markers

  • Alpha-fetoprotein (AFP): For liver cancer.
  • CA-125: For ovarian cancer.
  • Cancer antigens 15-3 and 27-29: For breast cancer.
  • C carcinoembryonic antigen (CEA): For various cancers.
  • Prostate-specific antigen (PSA): For prostate cancer.

Circulating Tumor Test

This relatively new test monitors certain types of cancer by looking for cancerous cells in the bloodstream.

Other Blood Tests

  • D-dimer test: Diagnoses blood clotting disorders.
  • Kappa or Lambda free light chain: Detects high protein levels related to amyloidosis or multiple myeloma.
  • Prothrombin time test (PTT): Diagnoses blood clotting disorders.
  • Reticulocyte count: Checks bone marrow's production of red blood cells.

When should I have a blood test?

Your healthcare provider will recommend tests based on your individual health needs. Annual physical examinations typically include a complete blood count test. Regular glucose tests may be suggested if you have overweight or obesity concerns.

Blood Test Preparation and Details

What should I do to prepare for my blood test?

Preparation for your blood test depends on the type of test you’re having. Here are some general guidelines:

  • Fasting: Some blood tests require fasting for several hours. Always check with your healthcare provider about specific instructions.
  • Hydration: If fasting isn't required, drink plenty of water to stay hydrated, which makes it easier to draw blood.
  • Moisturizer: Applying moisturizer on your arms can help the phlebotomist insert the needle more easily.
  • Gentle Exercise: Doing light exercise while waiting can help boost your blood pressure, making veins more accessible.

What happens during blood tests?

The process is typically performed by phlebotomists, who are trained to draw blood. Here’s what you can expect:

  1. You will sit in a chair with an armrest.
  2. If wearing long sleeves, roll them up past your elbow.
  3. The phlebotomist will clean the area with antiseptic and apply an elastic band around your arm.
  4. You may be asked to make a fist to encourage blood flow.
  5. A needle will be inserted quickly into your arm (venipuncture).
  6. The blood will flow into tubes for analysis, and multiple samples may be taken if necessary.
  7. After obtaining enough blood, the band will be removed and a bandage applied.

Are there different ways to do blood tests?

Yes, there are several methods for obtaining blood samples:

  • Venipuncture: The most common method involving drawing blood from a vein.
  • Finger Stick: A small prick on a finger to collect a tiny amount of blood.
  • Heel Stick: Commonly performed on newborns by pricking their heel.
  • Arterial Blood Gas Test: Blood is taken from an artery instead of a vein.

How much blood is taken during blood tests?

The amount varies by test; for example, a complete blood count (CBC) may require about 30 milliliters (mL) of blood. This is a small fraction compared to the total volume in an adult's body (4,500 to 5,700 mL).

Do blood tests hurt?

Pain levels can vary. Venipuncture usually causes mild discomfort, while arterial tests may be more painful. Always inform your phlebotomist if you experience discomfort.

I’m always anxious about having blood tests. What can I do to relax?

If you feel anxious, consider these coping strategies:

  • Understand the purpose of your tests.
  • Breathe deeply as the needle is inserted.
  • Avoid watching the procedure; look away instead.
  • Distract yourself by counting or engaging in conversation.

What happens after my blood test?

Your provider will place a bandage on the injection site and may recommend resting briefly before leaving.

Results and Follow-Up

When will I know my test results?

The timing varies based on the test type; some results are available within hours while others may take longer. Your provider will inform you how results will be communicated.

My healthcare provider wants to talk to me about my test results. Does that mean something’s wrong?

No, not necessarily. Discussions about results can occur even if they are part of routine checkups or if recommendations for health improvement are needed.

Blood tests are essential tools for monitoring health and diagnosing conditions. Your healthcare provider will explain the reasons for any recommended tests and their potential outcomes.
